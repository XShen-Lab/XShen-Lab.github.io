# Research

The XShen Lab studies how genomes generate diverse cellular identities through probabilistic molecular processes. We investigate the physics of nuclear structure and function, genome organization, transcription, noncoding RNA, and cell fate.

Our research integrates molecular and cellular biology, single-cell genomics, imaging, biochemical reconstitution, computational analysis, and theoretical modeling to uncover the organizing principles of nuclear function and genome information flow.

## Research Directions

### Physics of nuclear structure and function

We aim to understand how nuclear organization emerges from molecular interactions among DNA, RNA, proteins, and chromatin. A central question is how higher-order nuclear self-organization enables robust biological states across scales.

### Genome organization and chromatin architecture

We study how repetitive DNA sequences, including L1 and B1/Alu elements, contribute to three-dimensional genome organization. Our work explores how genomic sequence features encode structural rules for chromatin compartmentalization and nuclear architecture.

### Nuclear RNA and noncoding RNA regulation

We investigate how nuclear RNA and noncoding RNA regulate transcription, chromatin states, and genome organization. The lab studies both cis-regulatory functions of ncRNAs and systems-level roles of nuclear RNA homeostasis.

### Transcriptional regulation and phase separation

We study how RNA-binding proteins, RNA networks, and phase separation organize transcriptional machinery and regulate transcriptional output. This direction connects molecular mechanisms of transcription with chromatin state and cell identity.

### Single-cell nascent transcription and probabilistic genome usage

The lab develops and applies single-cell nascent RNA sequencing approaches, including scFLUENT-seq, to study genome activity in individual cells. These approaches reveal sparse and probabilistic genome usage, suggesting that stable cellular states emerge from collective patterns of stochastic transcription.

### Cell fate and cellular identity

We seek to understand how genome regulation, nuclear organization, and transcriptional dynamics drive cell fate decisions. This work connects nuclear mechanisms to development, stem cell biology, cell vitality, and disease-relevant cellular states.

## Experimental and Computational Approaches

- Single-cell genomics and nascent RNA sequencing
- Functional genomics and genome editing
- Imaging of nuclear organization and chromatin structure
- Biochemical and molecular reconstitution
- Computational analysis of genome, transcriptome, and chromatin data
- Theoretical modeling of nuclear function and cellular state emergence

## Representative Themes

- DNA-instructed chromatin organization
- Nuclear RNA homeostasis
- Noncoding RNA-mediated transcriptional regulation
- RNA-binding proteins and phase separation
- Sparse and probabilistic genome usage
- Genome information flow from DNA to RNA, proteins, and cellular phenotypes
