Research

Scientific Vision

The XShen Lab studies Biological Information Flow: From Genome to Cell Fate as a unified framework for understanding how stable biological order emerges from stochastic molecular activity. Although cell types share essentially the same genome, they acquire diverse and persistent identities while transcription, RNA metabolism, and chromatin organization continually fluctuate across space and time.

We propose that sparse and stochastic genome activity becomes stable biological information through four connected operations: organization, processing, selection, and stabilization. Our long-term goal is a predictive framework, grounded in physical principles and statistical theories, for understanding cellular states in development, aging, and disease.

Why This Question Matters

Two challenges remain central to modern molecular biology. Protein-coding genes occupy only a small fraction of the mammalian genome, leaving the organizing principles of the vast noncoding genome incompletely understood. At the same time, genome activity is intrinsically stochastic, whereas cellular identity and developmental programs are highly stable.

Biological information therefore cannot be understood as a simple DNA → RNA → protein pathway. It is continually organized, interpreted, and selected through genome organization, RNA networks, transcriptional dynamics, and surveillance. The noncoding genome contributes not only sequence context, but also nuclear architecture, RNA regulatory networks, and transcriptional environments. We ask not only which genes are expressed, but how biological systems organize, process, select, and stabilize information.

The Nucleus as a Dynamic Information-Processing System

The nucleus is a far-from-equilibrium polymer–electrolyte system containing two interconnected, charged, and heterogeneous polymer networks. Chromatin carries genetic information and provides the structural basis for genome folding. The RNA–RBP meshwork, composed of nascent RNA, RNA-binding proteins, and RNA-processing factors, helps determine when, where, and how genomic information is accessed.

High-resolution imaging suggests that chromatin transitions between compact globular nanodomains and extended coils. Inactive DNA tends to reside within globular domains, whereas actively transcribed DNA is exposed at domain surfaces or extended into coils that interact with the RNA–RBP meshwork. Their interface—the perichromatin region—is a dynamic surface where transcription, RNA processing, RNP assembly, RNA degradation, and nuclear transport are spatially and functionally coupled.

Transcription continually introduces RNA, energy, and entropy. In this active-matter view, biological information is shaped not by DNA sequence alone, but by the interplay of chromatin structure, RNA networks, and non-equilibrium dynamics.

Biological Information Flow

Organization → Processing → Selection → Stabilization

  • Organization: genome sequence and chromatin establish spatial information.
  • Processing: RNA networks create nuclear information-processing environments.
  • Selection: transcriptional surveillance promotes productive biological outputs.
  • Stabilization: probabilistic genome activities converge on stable cell identities.

Research Programs

1. Genome Organization

Organizing Biological Information

How does genome sequence encode nuclear organization?

We investigate how genome sequences establish nuclear architecture and influence gene regulation and cell fate. Our work shows that repetitive elements—including L1 and B1/Alu sequences—are not merely genomic byproducts; they encode principles of chromosome compartmentalization and nuclear positioning. We are extending this work to understand how repetitive elements and their RNA products shape chromatin organization, nuclear structures, and genome remodeling during development, cell-cycle transitions, and cell-fate changes.

2. RNA Networks

Processing Biological Information

How does RNA organize the nuclear environment?

RNA is both a carrier of genetic information and an organizer of nuclear function. Our studies established that noncoding RNAs can regulate neighboring genes and local chromatin states. We also found that RNA-binding proteins form dynamic RNA-associated condensates that regulate RNA Polymerase II recruitment, CTD phosphorylation, and transcriptional elongation. We now investigate how nascent RNA, RNA-binding proteins, and processing factors form an RNA–RBP meshwork that coordinates transcription, RNA maturation, RNA degradation, and nuclear organization.

3. Transcriptional Surveillance

Selecting Biological Information

How does productive biological information emerge from pervasive transcription?

Eukaryotic genomes are extensively transcribed, yet only a fraction of transcriptional events achieve productive elongation and generate mature mRNAs. We propose that productive transcription is an actively established state. Our ongoing work examines how the RNA Polymerase II CTD together with RNA-processing and degradation pathways forms a genome-scale surveillance system that eliminates nonproductive transcription while supporting RNA maturation, productive elongation, and polymerase recycling. Unpublished manuscript titles are not presented here.

4. Cell Fate Dynamics

Stabilizing Biological Information

How do probabilistic genome activities generate stable cell identity?

Stable identities need not arise from identical transcriptional programs executed by every cell. Using scFLUENT-seq, developed in our laboratory, we measure real-time genome usage at single-cell resolution. The publication-specific result shows that individual cells actively use a cell-type-dependent fraction of approximately 0.02%–3.1% of the genome at a given moment, while populations explore broader genomic landscapes. We model cellular identity as a stable probability distribution emerging from coordinated stochastic activity.

Technology Platforms

Biological Information Flow spans molecular dynamics, nuclear organization, and cellular states. We combine:

  • single-cell genomics;
  • live-cell and super-resolution imaging;
  • electron microscopy;
  • biochemical reconstitution;
  • CRISPR genome engineering;
  • computational analysis;
  • artificial intelligence;
  • theoretical modeling.

Theory-driven discovery is a defining feature of this work: quantitative experiments and models are developed together to uncover general principles across scales.

Future Vision

We aim to move beyond gene-centric and pathway-centric models toward a unified theory of Biological Information Flow. In this framework, DNA sequences encode genetic information and spatial organization; chromatin and RNA networks create information-processing environments; transcriptional surveillance selects productive information; and stable cellular states emerge from probabilistic genome activities.

Ultimately, we seek physical principles, statistical theories, and predictive models that make it possible to understand, predict, and control cellular states during development, aging, and disease.
